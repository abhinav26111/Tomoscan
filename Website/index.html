Bacterial Spot
Bacterial Spot

Symptoms:

  • Small, water-soaked spots on leaves that enlarge into brown lesions with yellow halos.
  • Lesions may develop a target-like appearance.
  • Spots may also appear on fruit, becoming raised and dark.

Causes:

  • Caused by bacteria (Xanthomonas spp.).
  • Spread through water, contaminated tools, or infected plant debris.

Cure/Treatment:

  • Remove and destroy infected plant material.
  • Apply copper-base liquid fungicides, Mancozeb, and chlorothalonil.
  • Copper products or copper plus mancozeb are registered and effective to control bacterial spot of tomato

Precautions:

  • Practice drip irrigation.
  • Practice crop rotation.
  • Avoid overhead watering to reduce humidity.
  • Keep garden clean and free of debris.
Early Blight
Early Blight

Symptoms:

  • Circular lesions with dark margins on lower leaves.
  • Lesions may have concentric rings, resembling a target.
  • Leaves may yellow and die prematurely.

Causes:

  • Fungal infection (Alternaria solani).
  • Thrives in warm, humid conditions.

Cure/Treatment:

  • Remove and destroy infected plant material.
  • Apply fungicides containing chlorothalonil or copper.
  • Custodia Fungicide- Azoxystrobin 11% + Tebuconazole 18.3% SC
  • Conika Fungicide- Kasugamycin 5% + Copper Oxychloride 45% WP
  • Kocide Fungicide- Copper Hydroxide 53.8% DF
  • Dhanuka M45 Fungicide- Mancozeb 75% WP

Precautions:

  • Rotate crops to prevent buildup of fungal spores.
  • Mulch around plants to reduce soil splashing.
Late Blight
Late Blight

Symptoms:

  • Water-soaked lesions on leaves, usually starting at leaf edges.
  • Lesions turn dark brown or black and become necrotic.
  • White mold may appear on the underside of leaves in humid conditions.

Causes:

  • Fungal infection (Phytophthora infestans).
  • Thrives in cool, moist weather.

Cure/Treatment:

  • Remove and destroy infected plant material.
  • Apply fungicides containing copper or chlorothalonil.
  • Bravo (chlorothalonil) and Dithane (mancozeb) are contact fungicides that need to be sprayed.

Precautions:

  • Space plants adequately for good air circulation.
  • Avoid watering foliage, water at soil level.
Leaf Mold
Leaf Mold

Symptoms:

  • Yellowish patches on leaves that become pale or grayish.
  • Fuzzy white or gray mold growth on the underside of leaves.
  • Leaves may curl or distort.

Causes:

  • Fungal infection (Fulvia fulva).
  • Thrives in warm, humid conditions.

Cure/Treatment:

  • Remove and destroy infected plant material.
  • Apply copper-based fungicides preventively.

Precautions:

  • Provide good air circulation by spacing plants appropriately.
  • Avoid overhead watering.

Two-Spotted Spider Mites
Two-Spotted Spider Mites

Symptoms:

  • Fine webbing on leaves and between stems.
  • Yellow stippling on leaves.
  • Leaves may appear dusty.

Causes:

  • Infestation by spider mites (Tetranychus spp.).
  • Thrives in hot, dry conditions.

Cure/Treatment:

  • Spray plants with water to dislodge mites.
  • Apply insecticidal soap or neem oil.

Precautions:

  • Increase humidity by misting plants regularly.
  • Introduce natural predators like ladybugs.

Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus
Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus

Symptoms:

  • Yellowing and upward curling of leaves.
  • Leaf thickening and reduced size.
  • Stunted growth and reduced fruit production.

Causes:

  • Viral infection transmitted by whiteflies.
  • Thrives in warm, tropical regions.

Cure/Treatment:

  • There is no cure for the virus.
  • Remove and destroy infected plants to prevent spread.

Precautions:

  • Control whitefly populations with insecticidal soap or neem oil.
  • Use reflective mulch to deter whiteflies.
Tomato Mosaic Virus
Tomato Mosaic Virus

Symptoms:

  • Mottled yellow-green patterns on leaves.
  • Stunted growth and reduced yield.
  • Fruits may have dark, sunken areas.

Causes:

  • Viral infection transmitted through contact with infected plants or soil.
  • Can also be transmitted by infected tools or hands.

Cure/Treatment:

  • There is no cure for the virus.
  • Remove and destroy infected plants.

Precautions:

  • Practice strict hygiene to avoid spreading the virus.
  • Control aphid populations, which can transmit the virus.
Healthy Tomato Leaf
Healthy Tomato Leaf

Description:

A healthy tomato leaf is typically green in color, with no signs of discoloration, spots, or abnormalities.

Features:

  • Uniform green color
  • No spots or discoloration
  • No signs of disease or pest damage
  • Firm and turgid texture

Maintenance:

To maintain healthy tomato leaves, ensure adequate watering, proper nutrition, and regular inspection for signs of disease or pest infestation.

Disease Prevention:

  • Practice crop rotation to prevent the buildup of soil-borne pathogens.
  • Provide good air circulation by spacing plants appropriately.
  • Avoid overhead watering to reduce humidity and minimize the risk of fungal diseases.
  • Inspect plants regularly for early signs of disease and promptly remove any infected foliage.
  • Use disease-resistant tomato varieties whenever possible.
